The Three Principal Verb Forms

Most English verbs have three main forms (also called principal parts): the base form, the past form, and the past participle form.

Base form: walk / Past: walked / Past Participle: walked Regular verb - past and past participle forms are identical
Base form: go / Past: went / Past Participle: gone Irregular verb - all three forms are different
Base form: see / Past: saw / Past Participle: seen Irregular verb - past and past participle forms different
Base Form (Infinitive)

The base form (also called the infinitive) is the form found in the dictionary and is used after 'to' in the full infinitive.

I want to study English. Used with 'to' in full infinitive
You should help your sister. Used after modal verbs without 'to'
They make me laugh. Used as bare infinitive after certain verbs
Past Form (Simple Past)

The past form is used to talk about actions or situations in the past. For regular verbs, we add -ed to the base form.

She worked yesterday. Regular verb: add -ed to base form
I saw a movie last night. Irregular verb: unique past form
They went to Paris last summer. Irregular verb: unique past form
Past Participle

The past participle is used to form perfect tenses and passive voice. For regular verbs, it's the same as the past form (-ed).

I have finished my homework. Used in present perfect tense
The window was broken by the storm. Used in passive voice
She had already eaten when I arrived. Used in past perfect tense
Common Irregular Verbs

Irregular verbs don't follow the pattern of adding -ed for past and past participle forms.

be → was/were → been One of the most irregular verbs in English
do → did → done Used as main verb and as auxiliary verb
eat → ate → eaten Different forms for all three principal parts
go → went → gone Completely different word for past form
have → had → had Past and past participle forms are identical
make → made → made Past and past participle forms are identical
see → saw → seen Different forms for all three principal parts
take → took → taken Different forms for all three principal parts
Regular Verbs

Regular verbs form their past tense and past participle by adding -ed (or sometimes just -d) to the base form.

work → worked → worked Simply add -ed to form both past and past participle
play → played → played For verbs ending in 'y' preceded by a vowel, just add -ed
talk → talked → talked Standard -ed ending
Spelling Rules for Regular Verbs

When adding -ed to regular verbs, there are some spelling rules to follow.

study → studied → studied For verbs ending in consonant + y: change y to i, then add -ed
stop → stopped → stopped For verbs ending in consonant + vowel + consonant: double the final consonant, then add -ed
like → liked → liked For verbs ending in 'e': just add -d
Irregular Verbs

Irregular verbs do not follow the regular pattern of adding -ed for past forms. Each has its own unique forms.

go → went → gone All three forms are different
bring → brought → brought Past and past participle forms are identical but don't follow -ed pattern
catch → caught → caught Vowel change plus -ght ending
read → read → read Spelled the same but pronounced differently
Categories of Irregular Verbs

Irregular verbs can be grouped into patterns that may help with memorization.

keep → kept, sleep → slept Verbs changing from 'ee' to 'ep'
sell → sold, tell → told Verbs changing from 'e' to 'o'
break → broke → broken, speak → spoke → spoken Verbs with similar vowel change patterns
Primary Auxiliary Verbs

Be, do, and have can function as main verbs or as auxiliary (helping) verbs.

I am studying English (be) Be as auxiliary in continuous tense
She does not like coffee (do) Do as auxiliary in negative sentences
They have finished their work (have) Have as auxiliary in perfect tense
Forms of Be

Be is the most common auxiliary verb and has various forms depending on tense and subject.

Formula:

  • Present: am (I), is (he/she/it), are (you/we/they)
  • Past: was (I/he/she/it), were (you/we/they)
I am studying English (be) Present continuous with 'am'
She was studying when I called. Past continuous with 'was'
The documents are being processed. Passive voice with 'are being'
English is spoken worldwide. Passive voice with 'is'
Forms of Do

Do is used to form questions and negative statements in simple present and past tenses.

Formula:

  • Present: do (I/you/we/they), does (he/she/it)
  • Past: did (all subjects)
Do you speak English? Question in present simple
She does not work here. She does not work here
Did they arrive on time? Question in past simple
We did not see the movie. Negative in past simple
Forms of Have

Have is used to form perfect tenses.

Formula:

  • Present: have (I/you/we/they), has (he/she/it)
  • Past: had (all subjects)
I have lived here for ten years. Present perfect with 'have'
She has already left. Present perfect with 'has'
They had finished dinner before we arrived. Past perfect with 'had'
Modal Verbs

Modal verbs express necessity, possibility, permission, ability, or obligation. They are followed by the base form of the main verb (without 'to').

I can swim. Ability: can
You should study more. Advice/recommendation: should
She must finish this today. Obligation/necessity: must
Can/Could

Used to express ability, possibility, or to ask for permission.

I can speak three languages/I could swim when I was five Present ability/Past ability
Can you help me with this? Request for help
Could I borrow your pen? Polite request (more formal than 'can')
Must/Have to

Used to express obligation, necessity, or strong probability.

Students must wear uniforms at this school. Strong obligation (rule)
I have to finish this report by tomorrow. External obligation (necessity)
She must be tired after working all night. Logical deduction - strong probability
Should/Ought to

Used to express advice, recommendation, or expectation.

You should exercise regularly. Advice or recommendation
We should arrive by 9:00 PM. Expectation
You should have called me. Past recommendation that didn't happen
May/Might

Used to express possibility or permission.

It may rain later today. Possibility (more certain than 'might')
They might not come to the party. Possibility (less certain)
May I come in? Asking for permission (formal)
Will/Would

Used to express future actions, willingness, or polite requests.

I will help you tomorrow. Future promise or intention
Would you mind closing the door? Polite request
She said she would call me. Future in the past
