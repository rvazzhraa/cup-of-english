Nouns

Nouns are words that name people, places, things, or ideas.

The teacher gave us homework. Common nouns: teacher, homework (general names)
London is the capital of England. Proper nouns: London, England (specific names, always capitalized)
Love and happiness are important in life. Abstract nouns: love, happiness (ideas or concepts)
I need three apples. Count nouns: can be counted (one apple, two apples)
She drinks water every day. Non-count nouns: cannot be counted (not 'one water, two waters')
The dog's toy is under the table. Possessive form: add 's to show ownership
Verbs

Verbs are words that show action or state of being.

She runs every morning. Action verb:shows physical action (runs)
He seems happy today. Linking verb: connects subject to description (seems)
I talk, I talked, I have talked. Regular verb: forms past tense by adding -ed
I go, I went, I have gone. Irregular verb: changes form in past tense
She is singing in the choir. Auxiliary verb + main verb:is (auxiliary) + singing (main)
They do not like seafood Auxiliary verb in negation:do not + like
Subject + Verb Agreement

In English, the subject and verb must agree in number (singular or plural).

She walks to school every day. Singular subject = singular verb
They walk to school every day. Plural subject (they) with plural verb (walk)
Articles (a, an, the)

Articles are used before nouns to indicate whether they are specific or general.

I saw a dog in the park. a dog (any dog, not specific)
The dog was barking loudly. the dog (specific dog)
She plays an instrument. an before words starting with vowel sounds
Plurals

Most nouns form their plurals by adding -s or -es to the singular form.

One book, two books Add -s to most nouns
One box, two boxes Add -es to nouns ending in -s, -ss, -sh, -ch, -x, -z
One child, two children Some nouns have irregular plurals
Pronouns

Pronouns replace nouns to avoid repetition. They include personal, possessive, and demonstrative pronouns.

I gave the book to him. Personal pronouns:I, you, he, she, it, we, they, me, him, her, us, them
This is my car. That is yours. Possessive pronoun:mine, yours, his, hers, its, ours, theirs
This apple is sweet. Those apples are sour. Demonstrative pronouns:this, that, these, those
Adjective Order

When multiple adjectives are used to describe a noun, they generally follow a specific order.

Prepositions

Prepositions show relationships between nouns/pronouns and other words in a sentence, typically indicating position, direction, or time.

The book is on the table. Position: on, in, under, above, below, between
She walked through the door. Direction:to, from, across, through
We'll meet at 6 o'clock. Time:at, in, on, during, before, after
Conjunctions

Conjunctions connect words, phrases, or clauses.

I like tea and coffee. Coordinating conjunctions: for, and, nor, but, or, yet, so
I'll go to the park if it doesn't rain. Subordinating conjunctions: because, since, as, although, though, if, when, etc.
Question Formation

Questions in English typically start with auxiliary verbs or question words (wh-words).

Do you speak English? Yes/No questions start with auxiliary verbs: do, does, did, is, are, etc.
Where do you live? Wh-questions start with question words: what, where, when, who, why, how
Negation

Negative sentences in English are formed by adding 'not' after auxiliary verbs or by using negative words.

She does not like coffee. Using 'not' with auxiliary verbs: do not (don't), does not (doesn't), etc.
I have never been to Paris. Using negative words: never, no, nobody, nothing, nowhere
Zero Conditional

Used for general truths or scientific facts - If + present simple, present simple.

If you heat water to 100¬∞C, it boils Scientific fact
If you don't water plants, they die. General truth
First Conditional

Used for possible future situations - If + present simple, will + infinitive.

If it rains tomorrow, we will stay at home. Possible future situation
If you study hard, you will pass the exam. Likely result in the future
Second Conditional

Used for hypothetical or unlikely situations - If + past simple, would + infinitive.

If I won the lottery, I would travel the world. Unlikely/hypothetical situation
If she had more time, she would learn Japanese. Hypothetical present situation
Third Conditional

Used for impossible past situations - If + past perfect, would have + past participle.

If I had studied harder, I would have passed the exam. Past situation that didn't happen
If they had left earlier, they wouldn't have missed the train. Impossible past situation
Participles

Participles are verb forms used as adjectives or to construct verb tenses. They include present participles (-ing) and past participles (-ed/irregular).

She is walking to school. Present participle (-ing): Used in continuous tenses ("is walking") or as adjectives (The running water was cold.)
I have finished my homework. Past participle (-ed/irregular): Used in perfect tenses ("have finished"), passive voice (The book was written) or as adjectives (The broken vase)
Walking to school, I saw my friend. Participial phrase: A group of words with a participle that functions as an adjective (modifies "I").
Exhausted from the journey, we fell asleep. (Past participle phrase modifies "we")
Agreements

The subject and verb must agree in number (singular/plural). Here are key rules with examples:

The dog barks. / The dogs bark. Basic Rule: Singular subjects take singular verbs; plural subjects take plural verbs.
Mary and John are coming. / Either the teacher or the students are responsible. Compound Subjects:
  • Use plural verbs for subjects joined by and
  • With or the verb agrees with the nearest subject.
The team is winning. / The jury were unable to agree. Collective Nouns:
  • Singular verbs if the group acts as one unit (is)
  • Plural verbs if members act individually (were) common in British English
Everyone has arrived. / Few were prepared. Indefinite Pronouns:
  • Singular verbs for everyone, nobody, each (has)
  • Plural verbs for few, many, several (were)
Passive & Active

Voice indicates whether the subject performs or receives the action of the verb. Here are the key differences and uses:

The chef cooked the meal. / Students are writing essays. Active Voice:
  • The subject performs the action.
  • Structure: Subject + Verb + Object
  • More direct and concise.
The meal was cooked by the chef. / Essays are being written by the students. Passive Voice:
  • The subject receives the action.
  • Structure: Subject + Be + Past Participle (+ by-agent)
  • Used when the doer is unknown, unimportant, or to emphasize the receiver
My car was stolen. / The patient will be examined. When to Use Passive Voice:
  • Unknown doer: "My car was stolen." (who stole it is unknown)
  • Unimportant doer: "This building was constructed in 1950." (the builder isn't relevant)
  • Emphasize the receiver: "The patient will be examined immediately." (focus on the patient)
Capitalization

English has specific rules for when to use capital letters.

My birthday is in January. Capitalize: First word of a sentence, days, months, holidays
I visited London last year. Capitalize: Names of people, places, nationalities, languages
I really like All Of Us Are Dead drama. Capitalize: Titles of books, movies, etc. (main words)
Present Simple Tense

Used for habits, facts, general truths, and scheduled events.

Formula:

  • Affirmative: Subject + base verb (+ s/es for 3rd person singular)
  • Negative: Subject + do/does + not + base verb
  • Question: Do/Does + subject + base verb?
I work in a bank. Regular fact/habit
The sun rises in the east. General truth
He doesn‚Äôt like coffee. Negative form with does + not
Do you speak English? Question form with do/does
Present Continuous Tense

Used for actions happening now, temporary situations, and future arrangements.

Formula:

  • Affirmative: Subject + am/is/are + verb-ing
  • Negative: Subject + am/is/are + not + verb-ing
  • Question: Am/Is/Are + subject + verb-ing?
She is studying for her exam. Action happening now
They are living in Paris for a year. Temporary situation
I'm meeting John at 6 PM. Future arrangement
Present Perfect Tense

Used for actions that started in the past and continue to the present, recent completed actions, or past experiences with present relevance.

Formula:

  • Affirmative: Subject + have/has + past participle
  • Negative: Subject + have/has + not + past participle
  • Question: Have/Has + subject + past participle?
I have lived in London for five years. Action started in past and continues to present
She has already finished her homework. Completed action with relevance to present
Have you ever visited Paris? Past experience with relevance to present
Present Perfect Continuous Tense

Used for actions that started in the past, continue to the present, and may continue into the future, often emphasizing duration.

Formula:

  • Affirmative: Subject + have/has + been + verb-ing
  • Negative: Subject + have/has + not + been + verb-ing
  • Question: Have/Has + subject + been + verb-ing?
I have been working here for ten years. Continuous action from past to present
She has been studying all day. Ongoing action with emphasis on duration
Have you been waiting long? Question about ongoing action
Past Simple Tense

Used for completed actions in the past, especially with a specific time reference.

Formula:

  • Affirmative: Subject + verb in past tense (regular: +ed, irregular: 2nd form)
  • Negative: Subject + did + not + base verb
  • Question: Did + subject + base verb?
I visited my grandmother last weekend. Completed past action with time reference
She didn't go to the party. Negative form with did + not
Did you see that movie? Question about past event
Past Continuous Tense

Used for actions in progress at a specific time in the past, often interrupted by another action.

Formula:

  • Affirmative: Subject + was/were + verb-ing
  • Negative: Subject + was/were + not + verb-ing
  • Question: Was/Were + subject + verb-ing?
I was watching TV when the phone rang. Action in progress interrupted by another action
They were playing tennis at 5 PM yesterday. Action in progress at a specific past time
Was she sleeping when you called? Question about action in progress
Past Perfect Tense

Used for actions that were completed before another action in the past, establishing a sequence of past events.

Formula:

  • Affirmative: Subject + had + past participle
  • Negative: Subject + had + not + past participle
  • Question: Had + subject + past participle?
I had finished my homework before my friends arrived. The homework was completed first, then friends arrived
She had never seen snow before she moved to Canada. One past action (seeing snow) happened before another past action (moving to Canada)
Had you eaten lunch before the meeting? Question about sequence of past events
Past Perfect Continuous Tense

Used for actions that were in progress for some time before another action in the past, emphasizing duration.

Formula:

  • Affirmative: Subject + had + been + verb-ing
  • Negative: Subject + had + not + been + verb-ing
  • Question: Had + subject + been + verb-ing?
I had been working for three hours when she called. Continuous action before another past action
They had been living in London for ten years before they moved to Paris. Extended duration before a change
Had you been waiting long before the bus arrived? Question about duration before past event
Simple Future Tense

Used for actions that will happen in the future, predictions, or spontaneous decisions.

Formula:

  • Affirmative: Subject + will + base verb
  • Negative: Subject + will + not + base verb
  • Question: Will + subject + base verb?
She will attend the meeting tomorrow. Planned future action
It will rain this afternoon. Prediction
I'll answer the phone. Spontaneous decision
Future Continuous Tense

Used for actions that will be in progress at a specific time in the future.

Formula:

  • Affirmative: Subject + will + be + verb-ing
  • Negative: Subject + will + not + be + verb-ing
  • Question: Will + subject + be + verb-ing?
At 8 PM tonight, I will be watching a movie. Action in progress at future time
They won't be working during the holidays. Negative form
Will you be traveling this weekend? Question about future plans
Future Perfect Tense

Used for actions that will be completed before a specific time in the future.

Formula:

  • Affirmative: Subject + will + have + past participle
  • Negative: Subject + will + not + have + past participle
  • Question: Will + subject + have + past participle?
By next year, I will have finished my degree. Completed action before future time
She won't have arrived by 6 PM. Negative form
Will you have completed the report by Friday? Question about completion
Future Perfect Continuous Tense

Used to emphasize the duration of an action up to a specific time in the future.

Formula:

  • Affirmative: Subject + will + have + been + verb-ing
  • Negative: Subject + will + not + have + been + verb-ing
  • Question: Will + subject + have + been + verb-ing?
By 2025, I will have been working here for 10 years. Duration up to future point
They won't have been waiting long when we arrive. Negative form
Will you have been studying long before the exam? Question about duration
Simple Past Future Tense

Used for future actions from a past perspective, often in reported speech.

Formula:

  • Affirmative: Subject + would + base verb
  • Negative: Subject + would + not + base verb
  • Question: Would + subject + base verb?
He said he would call me. Reported speech
I knew she wouldn't come. Negative form
Would you help me if I asked? Hypothetical question
Past Future Continuous Tense

Used for actions that would be in progress at a specific past-future time.

Formula:

  • Affirmative: Subject + would + be + verb-ing
  • Negative: Subject + would + not + be + verb-ing
  • Question: Would + subject + be + verb-ing?
She said she would be waiting at the cafe. Ongoing action from past perspective
I knew they wouldn't be working late. Negative form
Would you be traveling that weekend? Question about past-future plans
Past Future Perfect Tense

Used for actions that would have been completed before a past-future time.

Formula:

  • Affirmative: Subject + would + have + past participle
  • Negative: Subject + would + not + have + past participle
  • Question: Would + subject + have + past participle?
By 5 PM, he would have finished the project. Completion before past-future time
I knew she wouldn't have arrived yet. Negative form
Would you have completed the course by June? Question about past-future completion
Past Future Perfect Continuous Tense

Used to emphasize duration of an action up to a past-future time.

Formula:

  • Affirmative: Subject + would + have + been + verb-ing
  • Negative: Subject + would + not + have + been + verb-ing
  • Question: Would + subject + have + been + verb-ing?
By December, I would have been living here for 5 years. Duration up to past-future point
They wouldn't have been waiting long when we arrived. Negative form
Would you have been working there long before retiring? Question about past-future duration
